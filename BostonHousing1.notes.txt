📚 Background & Context

Origin: Collected by Harrison & Rubinfeld (1978)

Purpose: Study factors affecting housing prices in Boston-area suburbs

Observations: 506 census tracts

Type: Tabular, fully numeric → perfect for regression & correlation

Target variable: Median home value (MEDV)

It’s widely used in:

Linear & multiple regression

Feature correlation analysis

Model diagnostics and explainability demos

⚠️ Note: While still great for learning, the dataset is considered educational, not suitable for modern policy or fairness analysis.

🧭 Dataset Structure

Each row = one Boston-area census tract
Each column = a measured attribute of that tract

Column	Description	Units / Meaning
CRIM	Per capita crime rate	Higher = more crime
ZN	Proportion of residential land zoned for large lots	% of land
INDUS	Proportion of non-retail business acres	%
CHAS	Charles River dummy variable	1 = tract borders river
NOX	Nitric oxides concentration	Parts per 10 million
RM	Average number of rooms per dwelling	Continuous
AGE	Proportion of owner-occupied units built before 1940	%
DIS	Distance to employment centers	Weighted distance
RAD	Accessibility to radial highways	Index
TAX	Property tax rate	Per $10,000
PTRATIO	Pupil–teacher ratio	Continuous
B	Proportion of Black residents	See formula below
LSTAT	% lower status population	Socioeconomic
MEDV	Median home value	$1000s (target)
🧮 Important Index Explanations
🔹 RAD — Highway Accessibility Index

Higher values = closer/more access to major highways

Discrete integer scale

Strongly correlated with TAX and often negatively with MEDV

🔹 DIS — Distance Index

Weighted distance to 5 Boston employment centers

Higher = farther from jobs

Often shows negative correlation with price in urban economics

🔹 B — Racial Index (Historical)

Defined as:

B = 1000 * (Bk − 0.63)²


Where Bk = proportion of Black residents

⚠️ This variable is historical and controversial.
It reflects 1970s modeling assumptions, not modern standards.

👉 Many instructors now:

Exclude it

Use it only to discuss bias in datasets

🔹 LSTAT — Lower Status Population

% of population considered lower socioeconomic status

One of the strongest negative correlations with MEDV

Extremely useful for regression demos

📈 Typical Correlation Patterns

You’ll almost always see:

Strong positive with MEDV

RM (more rooms → higher value)

Strong negative with MEDV

LSTAT

CRIM

NOX

Multicollinearity examples

RAD ↔ TAX

NOX ↔ INDUS

Perfect for:

Correlation heatmaps

Feature selection

Explaining multicollinearity issues

🧪 Why It’s Ideal for Learning Regression

✔ Clean numeric data
✔ Clear target variable
✔ Mix of strong & weak correlations
✔ Real-world interpretability
✔ Supports simple and multivariate regression


⚠️ Ethical & Practical Notes

Contains historical socioeconomic bias

Not recommended for modern policy or fairness-sensitive ML

Excellent for technical learning only
